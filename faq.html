Frequently Asked Questions

Got questions? I've got answers. Here's what people ask me most often.

Getting Started

What does a mortgage agent actually do?
I work for you, not a bank. Instead of offering you just one lender's products, I shop your mortgage across dozens of lenders to find the best rate and terms for your specific situation. I handle the paperwork, communicate with lenders, and guide you through the entire process. Think of me as your mortgage advocate—someone in your corner making sure you don't overpay.
How much does it cost to use your services?
For most clients, my services are completely free. I'm paid by the lender when your mortgage closes—not by you. In some cases involving alternative lenders or complex situations, there may be a broker fee, but I'll always discuss this with you upfront before you commit to anything. No surprises.
Why should I use a mortgage agent instead of going to my bank?
Your bank can only offer you their products. I have access to over 50 lenders—including your bank. This means I can compare rates across the market and find options you'd never see walking into a single branch. I also know which lenders are offering the best deals right now, which ones are flexible on specific issues, and how to present your application for the best chance of approval. It's like having an insider on your side.

Down Payments & Affordability

How much do I need for a down payment?
In Canada, the minimum down payment depends on the purchase price. For homes under $500,000, you need at least 5% down. For homes between $500,000 and $999,999, you need 5% on the first $500K and 10% on the portion above that. For homes $1 million and over, you need 20% down. There are also programs that can help with your down payment—let's talk about what might work for your situation.
What credit score do I need to get a mortgage?
Most A-lenders (banks and major lenders) look for a credit score of at least 620-680. However, if your score is lower, there are still options. B-lenders and alternative lenders work with lower credit scores, often down to 500 or even lower in some cases. The rate and terms will vary, but having less-than-perfect credit doesn't mean you can't get a mortgage. Let me take a look at your situation.
How do I know how much I can afford?
That's one of the first things we figure out together. I'll look at your income, debts, down payment, and current rates to determine what you can qualify for. But I'll also help you think about what you should borrow—which isn't always the same as what you can borrow. Your mortgage should fit your life, not stretch you to the breaking point. A pre-approval conversation will give you a clear picture.

Rates & Terms

Should I choose a fixed or variable rate?
It depends on your risk tolerance and the current rate environment. Fixed rates give you certainty—your payment stays the same for the term. Variable rates can save you money if rates stay flat or drop, but your payment can increase if rates rise. Historically, variable rates have often worked out better over the long run, but that's not guaranteed. I'll walk you through the current numbers and help you make an informed decision based on your situation.
What's the difference between term and amortization?
Your amortization is the total length of time to pay off your mortgage—typically 25 or 30 years. Your term is how long your current rate and conditions are locked in—commonly 5 years, but ranging from 1 to 10 years. At the end of each term, you renew (or refinance) for another term until the amortization is complete. Think of the term as chapters in a book, and the amortization as the whole book.
Can I pay off my mortgage faster?
Yes, most mortgages include prepayment privileges that let you pay extra without penalty—typically 10-20% of the original principal per year, plus the ability to increase your regular payments. These privileges vary by lender, and I always make sure your mortgage has good prepayment options. Small extra payments can shave years off your mortgage and save thousands in interest.

The Process

What documents do I need to apply for a mortgage?
The basics include: government-issued ID, proof of income (pay stubs, T4s, or tax returns if self-employed), proof of down payment, and information about your debts and assets. If you're purchasing, you'll also need the Agreement of Purchase and Sale. Don't worry—I'll give you a clear checklist and walk you through exactly what's needed. It's simpler than it sounds.
How long does the mortgage process take?
Pre-approval can often happen within 24-48 hours. Once you have an accepted offer on a home, final approval typically takes 3-5 business days, assuming all documents are in order. The full process from application to closing depends on your closing date, but I keep things moving and make sure there are no surprises along the way.
What's the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval?
Pre-qualification is a quick estimate based on information you provide—it's a ballpark figure but doesn't carry much weight. Pre-approval is more thorough: I verify your income, check your credit, and get actual approval from a lender for a specific amount. Pre-approval shows sellers you're a serious buyer and gives you confidence about your budget. I recommend getting pre-approved before you start house hunting.

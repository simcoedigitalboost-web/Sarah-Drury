Self-Employed Mortgages

Business owners, freelancers, and contractors—getting a mortgage doesn't have to be a nightmare. I specialize in making it work.

The Self-Employed Challenge

You run a successful business. You make good money. You pay your bills on time. But when you walk into a bank, they act like you're a risk.

Here's the problem: traditional lenders want to see T4s and steady paychecks. They don't understand how self-employment works. They look at your tax return—which your accountant has (legally) optimized to minimize taxes—and assume that's all you make.

Your declared income and your actual income are often two very different numbers. Banks don't always get that. I do.

I Know How to Present Your File

As a mortgage agent, I work with lenders who specialize in self-employed clients. More importantly, I know how to package your application in a way that makes sense to underwriters.

This might include:

  • Stated income programs — Some lenders will work with your stated income rather than just your tax returns
  • Bank statement programs — Showing consistent deposits over 12-24 months to demonstrate actual cash flow
  • Add-backs — Accounting for business expenses that don't reflect your true earning power
  • Business-for-self documentation — Presenting your business financials in the most favorable light

Every self-employed situation is unique. I'll look at your specific circumstances and figure out the best approach.

Who This Applies To

If any of these describe you, self-employed mortgage solutions might be right for your situation:

  • Sole proprietors and independent contractors
  • Small business owners (incorporated or not)
  • Freelancers and consultants
  • Commission-based sales professionals
  • Gig economy workers
  • Multiple income stream earners
  • Seasonal workers with irregular income

What You'll Need

Self-employed mortgages require more documentation than traditional applications, but I'll walk you through exactly what's needed. Typically, this includes:

  • Two years of personal tax returns (T1 Generals)
  • Notice of Assessments from CRA
  • Business financial statements or T2s (if incorporated)
  • 12-24 months of bank statements
  • Proof of business registration or articles of incorporation
  • Sometimes: a letter from your accountant

Don't worry if this seems like a lot—I'll give you a clear checklist and help you gather everything.

The Trade-Off

I'll be honest with you: self-employed mortgages sometimes come with slightly higher rates than traditional financing. The lender is taking on a bit more perceived risk, and they price accordingly.

However, the difference is often smaller than people expect. And for many self-employed clients, a slightly higher rate is far better than not getting approved at all—or not being able to buy the home you actually want.

I'll always show you all your options and explain the trade-offs clearly so you can make an informed decision.

Been Declined Before?

If a bank has already turned you down, don't give up. Banks have rigid criteria and limited options. I have access to lenders they don't even know about—lenders who specialize in exactly your situation.

A decline from one lender doesn't mean you can't get a mortgage. It might just mean you were talking to the wrong lender.

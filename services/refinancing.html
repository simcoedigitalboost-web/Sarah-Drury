Mortgage Refinancing

Your home's equity is sitting there doing nothing. Let's put it to work.

What Is Refinancing, Really?

Refinancing means replacing your current mortgage with a new one—usually to access your home's equity, get a better rate, or change your terms. If your home is worth more than you owe, that difference is equity you can use.

Think of it this way: your home has been quietly building value while you make payments. Refinancing lets you tap into that value without selling your home.

Why People Refinance

There's no single reason to refinance—it depends on what you're trying to accomplish. Here are the most common situations I help clients with:

Debt Consolidation
Credit cards, car loans, lines of credit—they all charge higher interest than a mortgage. If you're carrying high-interest debt, rolling it into your mortgage can dramatically reduce your monthly payments and total interest paid. One client saved over $800 a month by consolidating. That's not a typo.

Home Renovations
Want to renovate your kitchen, finish your basement, or add a deck? Instead of draining your savings or financing through a high-interest loan, refinancing lets you borrow against your home at mortgage rates. Plus, smart renovations can increase your home's value.

Investment Opportunities
Some homeowners use their equity to invest—whether that's buying a rental property, investing in the market, or starting a business. The interest on money borrowed for investment purposes may even be tax-deductible. (Talk to your accountant about the specifics.)

Better Rate or Terms
If rates have dropped significantly since you got your mortgage, or if your financial situation has improved, you might qualify for a better deal. Even a small rate reduction can save thousands over the life of your mortgage.

How Much Can You Access?

In Canada, you can typically refinance up to 80% of your home's current value. So if your home is worth $500,000 and you owe $300,000, you could potentially access up to $100,000 in equity ($500,000 × 80% = $400,000 – $300,000 owed = $100,000).

The actual amount depends on your income, credit, and the current appraisal of your home. I can help you figure out exactly what's possible in your situation.

The Costs to Consider

Refinancing isn't free. Before you decide, we need to weigh the costs against the benefits:

  • Prepayment Penalty — If you're breaking your mortgage early, there may be a penalty. This is often the biggest cost, especially with fixed-rate mortgages.
  • Legal Fees — You'll need a lawyer to register the new mortgage.
  • Appraisal Fee — The lender needs to confirm your home's current value.
  • Discharge Fee — Your current lender may charge a fee to discharge your existing mortgage.

Here's the thing: I'll calculate all of this for you upfront. Sometimes refinancing makes sense; sometimes it doesn't. I'll tell you honestly either way.

When Refinancing Makes Sense

Refinancing is usually a good idea when the savings or benefits significantly outweigh the costs. For example:

  • You're paying 19% on credit card debt but could pay 5% on your mortgage
  • You need funds for renovations that will increase your home's value
  • Interest rates have dropped substantially since your original mortgage
  • Your financial situation has improved and you now qualify for better terms

When It Might Not Make Sense

I'll be honest with you if refinancing isn't the right move:

  • The prepayment penalty wipes out your savings
  • You're close to renewal anyway (might be better to wait)
  • You don't have a clear plan for the funds
  • It would stretch your budget too thin

My job is to help you make the right decision, not just any decision.

The Process

Step 1: We talk about your goals and run the numbers. What are you trying to accomplish? How much equity do you have? What would the costs be?

Step 2: I shop for the best refinancing options across my network of lenders. Different lenders have different products and rates.

Step 3: We compare the options and make a decision together. You'll know exactly what you're getting and what it costs.

Step 4: I coordinate the paperwork, appraisal, and legal process. You sign, the funds flow, and your new mortgage is in place.

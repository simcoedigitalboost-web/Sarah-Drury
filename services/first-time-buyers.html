Most First-Time Buyers Leave Money on the Table

Here's what usually happens: you walk into a bank, they run some numbers, and they offer you a mortgage. You assume that's the best you can do. You sign. Done.

But here's what you don't see: there are grants, rebates, incentives, and programs specifically designed to help first-time buyers—and most people never hear about them. Between government programs, lender incentives, and tax breaks, you could be looking at $40,000 or more in savings over the life of your mortgage.

That's not an exaggeration. That's what happens when you know where to look.

Programs You Need to Know About

First-Time Home Buyer Incentive

The government provides 5-10% of your home's purchase price toward your down payment as a shared equity mortgage. This reduces your monthly payments without you having to save more upfront.

First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

A tax-free savings account specifically for your down payment. Contributions are tax-deductible (like an RRSP) and withdrawals for your home are tax-free (like a TFSA). Best of both worlds.

Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)

Withdraw up to $35,000 from your RRSP tax-free to put toward your down payment. Couples can each withdraw $35,000—that's $70,000 from your retirement savings to get into your home now.

Land Transfer Tax Rebate

Ontario first-time buyers can get a rebate of up to $4,000 on land transfer tax. That's money back in your pocket at closing.

Down Payment Assistance Programs

Various programs exist to help buyers who don't have a full down payment saved. I'll help you find out if you qualify and guide you through the application process.

Cash-Back Mortgages

Some lenders offer cash back at closing—money you can use for moving costs, furniture, or immediate renovations. I know which lenders offer the best cash-back deals.

Home Warranty Programs

Qualify for free home warranty coverage worth up to $10,000, protecting you against unexpected repairs in your first years of ownership.

New to Canada Program

Recently immigrated? Special mortgage programs exist for newcomers with limited Canadian credit history. You can qualify with as little as 5% down.

Down Payment: What You Actually Need

The 20% down payment myth stops a lot of people from even trying to buy. Here's the reality:

  • Under $500,000: Minimum 5% down
  • $500,000 to $999,999: 5% on first $500K, 10% on the rest
  • $1 million and up: 20% minimum

So for a $400,000 home, you could get in with $20,000 down. Combine that with the Home Buyers' Plan and down payment assistance programs, and homeownership might be closer than you think.

Understanding the Stress Test

You've probably heard about the mortgage stress test. Here's what it means: even if you're getting a mortgage at 5%, you have to prove you could afford payments at around 7% (the qualifying rate changes periodically).

This affects how much you can borrow. The good news? I can run these numbers for you before you start shopping, so you know your real budget—not what online calculators tell you.

Credit Score: Where You Need to Be

Most A-lenders want to see a credit score of at least 620-680. If you're below that, we still have options—but the best rates require good credit.

If your score needs work, don't worry. I'll give you specific, actionable tips to improve it. Sometimes a few months of targeted effort can make a significant difference in what rates you qualify for.
